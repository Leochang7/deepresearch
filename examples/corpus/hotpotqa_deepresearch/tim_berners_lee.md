# Tim Berners-Lee

Sir Timothy John Berners-Lee, born on June 8, 1955, in London, England, is a British computer scientist best known as the inventor of the World Wide Web. In 1989, while working at CERN in Geneva, Switzerland, he proposed a system for information management that became the foundation of the web.

Berners-Lee attended the University of Oxford, where he studied physics at The Queen's College. He graduated with a first-class degree in Physics in 1976. After Oxford, he worked as an engineer at Plessey, a telecommunications company in Poole, before moving on to various roles in computing.

In 1990, Berners-Lee developed the first web browser and web server, writing the code on a NeXT computer at CERN. He also created the first website, which went live on August 6, 1991. He wrote the first version of HTML, URI, and HTTP specifications that remain the foundation of the web today.

Berners-Lee founded the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) in 1994 at MIT, where he serves as director. He was knighted by Queen Elizabeth II in 2004 for his pioneering work. He is a professor at MIT and also holds a professorship at the University of Oxford.
